Judicial Push for Environmental CSR
How the Indian judiciary is reshaping corporate environmental responsibility — from voluntary charity to constitutional mandate.

By Jasleen Kaur · MBA, Development & Sustainable Finance

A Paradigm Shift
The Indian judiciary is actively reshaping the landscape of Corporate Social Responsibility by increasingly framing environmental spending not as voluntary charity but as a constitutional and legal mandate. This represents a significant evolution from the traditional understanding of CSR under the Companies Act, 2013, which has historically seen environmental causes sidelined in favor of education, healthcare, and rural development.

Key Judicial Developments
Five landmark judicial actions that are redefining corporate environmental accountability in India.

Supreme Court's Constitutional Reinterpretation
The Court invoked Article 51A(g) — a fundamental duty to protect the natural environment — to argue that the right to conduct business is inseparably linked to the responsibility to restore the planet. Catalyzed by cases involving the Great Indian Bustard, this reframes environmental spending as a constitutional obligation.

Post-Facto Environmental Clearance Debate
A landmark matter concerns the validity of retrospective environmental clearances. A two-judge bench struck down 2017/2021 notifications as unconstitutional; a three-judge bench (2:1) reversed this, noting demolition of ₹20,000+ crore projects. The matter may be referred to a five-judge Constitution Bench.

Afforestation as a Core Judicial Priority
In February 2026, the Supreme Court emphasized that increasing green cover is the only sustainable long-term solution to Delhi's air pollution. The Forest Research Institute projected ₹3.69 crore for greening and ₹4.43 crore for a complete tree census.

High Court Activism: Solid Waste Management
The Himachal Pradesh High Court applied the "Polluter Pays" principle — ordering strict action against bottling units, mandating recovery of ₹10 crore in garbage user charge arrears, and enforcing the Deposit Refund Scheme, 2025.

NGT Enforcement Actions
The NGT directed ₹50.10 lakh environmental damage compensation in the Kolhapur Sugar Mill case (March 2026) and imposed ₹25,000 cost on DUSIB for failing to file status reports in the Delhi Barapullah Drain case.

"India contributes not even 10% to global warming, yet the burden of environmental protection falls disproportionately on domestic courts, even as high-carbon-footprint nations remain indolent and indifferent."
— Chief Justice Surya Kant's Bench, Supreme Court of India

The Restoration Gap
India aims to restore 26 million hectares of degraded land by 2030 under the Bonn Challenge. However, private companies have contributed a mere 2% to the 9.8 million hectares restored so far.

Legislative Context
Jan Vishwas Act, 2023
De-criminalized violations under the Environment Protection Act, 1986. Critics argue this allows defaulters to escape with minor penalties, undermining environmental enforcement. Under active judicial scrutiny.

2017 & 2021 Notifications
The 2017 Notification granted exemption from prior environmental clearance requirements. The 2021 Office Memorandum extended the pathway for project regularization. Both remain under active Supreme Court scrutiny.

Way Forward

Conclusion
The Indian judiciary is actively redefining the boundaries of corporate environmental responsibility, moving from a voluntary framework to one grounded in constitutional duty. As the Supreme Court considers referral of key environmental questions to a Constitution Bench, and as High Courts and the NGT continue their enforcement actions, the trajectory points toward greater judicial oversight of corporate environmental conduct.

For businesses operating in India, this signals that environmental stewardship can no longer be treated as an afterthought to social development priorities but must be integrated as a core component of corporate accountability.
